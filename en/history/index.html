Prologue

Some places
are not built.
They are summoned.

“We came looking for a building. We found something that had spent decades waiting to be woken up.”

Not every idea is born from a blueprint or a boardroom. Some come from an obsession — from the unsettling certainty that two apparently incompatible worlds (the ceremonial precision of Japanese street cooking and the captivating darkness of horror) could not only coexist, but were always destined to meet.

This is the story of El Sanatorio. Not the one you’ll read in a press release. The real one.

The Concept

Japanese street food
for restless souls

Yakitori is, by nature, democratic. It was born in the alleys of Tokyo, under paper lanterns, in the perfumed smoke of charcoal grills. Labourers, clerks, poets and drifters shared the same high stools, the same skewers of thigh and skin, the same cheap bottle of sake that made honest conversation possible.

Horror, by contrast, was born of what humans cannot control. The fear of the dark, of what waits on the other side, of houses with histories, of corridors that creak. But also — and very few people admit this — from a deep fascination. From that irresistible urge to look over the edge of the abyss.

At El Sanatorio we do not treat horror as decoration. We treat it as language. A language we use to build atmosphere, narrative and experience — because a good meal and a good ghost story share the same structure: tension, development, release, and a conclusion you don’t forget.

“We didn’t want to build a themed restaurant. We wanted to build a place that had a soul. Even if that soul was a little dark.”
— The founders, on the origin of the project
The Space

An old hospital
that refused
to be silenced

When the team at The Maia Group started looking for a location, the brief was specific: a space with character, with layers, with history visible on its walls. Nothing built yesterday. Nothing antiseptic. Something that already kept its own secrets.

What we found exceeded any expectation. The former Hospital del Tórax — annex of the historic Hospital San Juan de Dios — had passed through decades of clinical life, of abandonment, and of rebirth. High ceilings with worn mouldings. Floors that hold memory under their layers of paint. Windows that look out like eyes that have watched for too long.

The design decision was radical from the start: not to restore in order to erase the past, but to intervene in order to amplify it. Cracks that tell stories were left visible. Dark corners became deliberate corners. Architectural imperfection turned into aesthetic virtue.

The result is a space where the Japanese and the old clinical architecture speak without any apparent friction. The dark-wood counters, the skewers over the embers, the amber light of the modernised oil lamps — all of it lives alongside the century-old arches, the high ceilings, and that persistent feeling that the building has more to say than we are prepared to hear.

Our Philosophy

What we believe

We believe eating well should never be boring.
We believe that fear well administered is pleasure.
We believe the street has always known more than the elegant restaurant.
We believe that darkness has its own form of beauty.
We believe that an ember and a good skewer can be, in the right context, something close to sacred.

At El Sanatorio, the experience doesn’t begin when you sit at the table. It begins when you cross the door, when your nose picks up the first trace of binchotan, when your eyes take a second to adjust to the light. The horror here is subtle. It’s not a mask that jumps at you. It’s a presence. A weather. An atmosphere that stays with you all night.

And when you leave — fingertips lacquered with tare, ears still carrying one of the ambient sounds that haunt the place — something inside you will have shifted, just a little. That’s what we’re after. Not just that you eat well, but that you remember being here.

Timeline

How we got
here

Every project worth anything follows a non-linear path. El Sanatorio was no exception. It was a string of hunches, discoveries and decisions made in the dark — metaphorically, and sometimes literally.

The Obsession

Yakitori as the starting point

It all started with a trip. One night in an alley in Osaka, under paper lanterns, discovering that a charcoal fire and a skewer could generate an experience as intense as any tasting menu. Simplicity as the highest form of complexity.

The Fusion

When horror met the grill

The question almost asked itself: what would happen if the experience of a dim izakaya, the heavy atmosphere and the visible fire, were wrapped in the aesthetics of horror? Not gore — psychological. Atmospheric. The pleasant discomfort of the unknown.

The Space

The building that chose them

The first time the Maia Group team saw the former Hospital del Tórax, the decision was instant. It didn’t need to be sold. It spoke for itself. It had decades of silence stored up, ready to be interrupted — in the right way.

The Opening

El Sanatorio opens its doors

The concept took shape. The coals were lit. The first tables filled with people who arrived curious and left with something harder to put into words. A taste on the tongue, and a story to tell.

Today

The place keeps evolving

El Sanatorio is not static. The menu changes. Events evolve. Corners of the space rearrange. Like anything alive — or pretending to be alive — it does not stay the same two nights in a row.

The Group

Part of something
larger

El Sanatorio wasn’t born in a vacuum. It’s part of The Maia Group, a collection of hospitality concepts founded on a simple but radical premise: no restaurant should look like another.

The Maia Group doesn’t build chains. It builds universes. Every concept inside the group has its own identity, its own narrative, its own visual and culinary language. What ties them together isn’t aesthetics — it’s a commitment to the radical experience. To the conviction that eating out can — and should — be memorable.

Inside that ecosystem, El Sanatorio is the darkest project, the riskiest, and perhaps the most honest. Because horror — done well — doesn’t lie. It doesn’t compromise. It doesn’t try to please everyone. And that, in hospitality, is exactly the right attitude.

Being part of The Maia Group means access to the resources, the network and the experience of a group with vision. It means that behind every decision — from the binchotan supplier to the menu design — there’s a standard of excellence that is not up for negotiation.

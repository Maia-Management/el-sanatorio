Document 01 · Recovered 7 Apr 1952

Admission Form — Patient No. 013

Found in boxes, children’s wing — Ward 3 archive 1952 · folio 013

An admission form for a young girl, approximately 6–8 years old, female. The name field is rendered illegible by water damage. Assigned to Ward 3 (Children's Wing). Patient No. 013. Notes describe her as quiet, cooperative, prone to softly repeating a song, and smiling often. Signed by Dr. H. Varón M. and Nurse R. Nieves.

Andrew & Luz · a margin note Luz checked the dates. The Hospital San Juan de Dios, right there in the Centro Histórico, had been running since 1746 — and from 1880 onwards it was run by nuns. The Sisters of the Presentation. And during La Violencia, between 1948 and 1958, nearly a million Colombians were displaced across the country. If a wealthy Magdalena family had a “difficult” girl they wanted to hide, they didn’t send her to the nuns’ hospital. They sent her to Calle 19. The Old Hospital San Juan de Dios — Wikipedia
Document 02 · Recovered 14 Mar 1953

Nurse Rosalba Nieves’s Diary — Fragment

Brown-ink handwriting · gridded paper nieves diary · I/III

The patient in Ward 3 keeps singing. Today it was "Arroz con leche" for three hours without stopping. I asked her to rest. She looked at me, smiled, and kept singing. She won't eat. We brought soup and bread. She only drank the water. But she smiles. She always smiles. I asked her if she missed her family. She said, "Which family?" Sometimes I think this place is the only thing she has ever known.

Andrew & Luz · a margin note This entry haunts me. There’s another Santa Marta story about a nun they call La Monja del Pasillo (The Nun of the Corridor) — she lived and worked at the Hospital San Juan de Dios, fell for a doctor, wasn’t loved back, and hanged herself in one of the hallways. They say her ghost still walks the wards. Luz thinks Nieves knew her. That the nuns of the Centro Histórico talked among themselves. I have no proof — just Luz’s hunch. La Monja del Pasillo — Visit Santa Marta

Basement Protocol Notes — Fragment

"Night session #47. Subject [redacted]. Insulin coma: dose 80U. Convulsion 14 min. Partial waking 22:40. [redacted] no scream."

"Session #51. Repeat basement protocol. Ward 3 girl — do not include. Dr. Varón insists."

"Compound prepared in the central pharmacy. Route: intramuscular. Refrigeration required."

"[redacted] keeps singing upstairs. We hear her through the pipes. It is the only thing audible after the scream."

Andrew & Luz · a margin note I went down a rabbit hole reading about the “heroic therapies” of 1950s psychiatry — insulin coma, electroshock without anaesthesia, lobotomy. In Europe they were standard; in Latin America nobody was looking. The San Juan de Dios nuns — Sisters of the Presentation — would never have tolerated them. But that was the thing: if you wanted to do that kind of “treatment” in Santa Marta, you didn’t do it there. You did it in a private house on Calle 19, off the books. Luz says that explains the basement. 'Heroic therapies' in psychiatry — Science Museum
Document 04 · Recovered 14 Feb 1958

Complaint Record — Governor’s Office, Magdalena

Red stamp: “DISMISSED” — bleeding ink magdalena governor · 1958-073

An official complaint filed February 14, 1958, alleging mistreatment of patients, audible screams from the street, and chemical smells overnight at the Sanatorio Varón. Following an inspection on February 9, 1958, and citing Dr. Varón's standing among "honorable Departmental families," the Secretaría de Salud dismissed the complaint for lack of evidence. Archived.

Andrew & Luz · a margin note When we read this we thought of Pepe Vives de Andréis — several-time Governor of Magdalena, Mayor of Santa Marta, the man who finished building the modern San Juan de Dios. If your family had any link to the governor’s office, a complaint against a doctor friend got filed away the same day. It’s not theory — that’s how 1950s Magdalena worked. Luz thinks the “complainant” in the record was a relative of the patient. I think it was the patient herself, and that’s why they asked for the name to be scrubbed out. Pepe Vives y el San Juan de Dios — El Informador

Document 05 · Recovered 12 Sep 1961

Nurse Rosalba Nieves’s Diary — Final Entry

Hurried strokes · black ink · creased paper nieves diary · III/III

I can no longer stay in this place. What happens at night is not medicine. The screams from the basement are not from treatment. I have seen the marks. I have seen the eyes of those who come back up. The girl in Ward 3 is the only one who does not scream. She only sings. I think she is the only one who is happy here — or the only one who has forgotten how to be unhappy. I am leaving. May God forgive us all.

Andrew & Luz · a margin note Luz found this in a Universidad del Magdalena paper — by 1961, most rural nurses had already left for Bogotá or Medellín. So when Nieves wrote “I can’t stay here” that same year, it wasn’t just about the Sanatorio Varón. The whole region was bleeding medical staff. She wasn’t the only one running. But she was the only one who wrote it down before she went. Violence in Colombia: A Timeline — NACLA
Document 06 · Recovered 28 Oct 1963

Death Certificate — Dr. Hernando Varón Mejía

1st Notary · dry seals · stamped paper death · folio 1963-1142

Death certificate from the First Notary of Santa Marta, dated October 28, 1963. Dr. Hernando Varón Mejía, age 58, psychiatrist, died at 4:17 a.m. in his office at Calle 19 #4-23. Cause of death: cardiac arrest. No autopsy performed. Found by the on-duty nurse. Ward 3 reported empty at the same hour.

Andrew & Luz · a margin note “Cardiac arrest. No autopsy.” That line gets me every time. It was October ’63 in Santa Marta — a month before Kennedy was shot in Texas. Small private clinics closed when the owner died because the children didn’t want to inherit the problem. Luz says it must have been the on-shift nurse who found him. And that Ward 3 went empty at the same hour — the official certificate doesn’t say that. Someone wrote it afterwards, by hand, on the file. Who that was, we don’t know. Memorias de un sanatorio — SciELO Colombia
Document 07 · Recovered 30 Nov 1964

Closure Act — Sanatorio Varón

Hand-annotated margin · different ink closure act · governor 1964

Official closure act of the Sanatorio Varón, dated 30 November 1964, certifying cessation of operations from 15 November 1964. 11 patients transferred to Barranquilla and Bogotá, 3 discharged, 4 deceased during operations, 0 unresolved. A handwritten note in the margin, in different ink and an unknown hand, reads: "And the girl?"

Andrew & Luz · a margin note The margin note — “And the girl?” — is the last thing anyone wrote about her for sixty-one years. Five years earlier, in 1959, the Centro Histórico of Santa Marta had been declared a national heritage site. But heritage isn’t the same as care. The building was sealed. The question stayed there, in the margin of the act, with no answer. Until in January 2026 we went down to the basement. And we understood. Monumentos del Centro Histórico — Alcaldía de Santa Marta
Document 08 · Recovered Dec 1964

Newspaper Clipping — El Informador (Santa Marta)

Yellow paper · scissor-cut edge el informador · Dec 1964

Headline: "Sanatorium on Calle 19 closes its doors." Following Dr. Hernando Varón Mejía's death, his family has decided to permanently shut the psychiatric establishment that operated for sixteen years in the Centro Histórico. Remaining patients reassigned to institutions in Barranquilla. Neighbors expressed relief, saying the building "always had something strange about it" and that "things were heard at night." The Varón family declined to comment.

Andrew & Luz · a margin note Lo loco de este recorte es que El Informador apenas tenía seis años. Lo fundó Pepe Vives de Andréis en agosto del 58 — el mismo Pepe que terminó de construir el San Juan de Dios moderno. Es decir: el periódico del filántropo del hospital oficial publicando el cierre del sanatorio privado que operaba a tres cuadras. Los vecinos hablan "alivio." Pepe no comenta. Es chistoso porque también es triste. El Informador — Santa Marta

Andrew & Luz · a margin note Before the marijuana boom of the 1970s, the Centro Histórico collapsed. Sealed buildings, peeling façades, empty streets. The official San Juan de Dios stayed open until ’91 and then it too was abandoned for ten years. If the nuns’ hospital could sit empty in the middle of the centre, what do you think was happening to a private house no one was claiming any more? Nobody went up. Nobody knocked on the door. Sixty-one years. Luz says that silence was the care she chose. Memorias de violencia: Bonanza Marimbera en Santa Marta — JSTOR
Document 10 · Found 14 Jan 2026

Internal Memo — Renovation Team

Current paper · laser print · rusted staple site · memo 2026-01-014

TO: General Direction. FROM: Construction team. RE: Basement finding. During the cleaning of the basement, we found evidence of long-term habitation in the northwest area of the lower level. Specifically: improvised bedding (hospital cloth); hundreds of empty water containers; tally marks on the walls (groups of five, thousands of marks); children's drawings affixed to the wall with deteriorated adhesive. The drawings show a progression: the first are clearly from a small girl (crayon, simple figures). The last show the same hand but aged, trembling. All drawings show the same image: a woman in white (nurse?) and a smiling girl. Awaiting instructions on how to proceed.

Andrew & Luz · a margin note We spent that whole week not knowing what to do. At the San Juan de Dios — the official one, the nuns’ one — they still say La Monja del Pasillo appears, the one who hanged herself for love in the corridors. They never did anything to that building. They let her walk. When we saw the thousands of tally marks in the basement on Calle 19, Luz asked: “What if we don’t have to do anything either?” I didn’t know what to answer. That’s why we called Dr. Silvio that night. La Monja del Pasillo — Visit Santa Marta
Document 11 · Found in Ward 3 date unknown

Patient File — Final Entry

The ink is fresh. The paper isn’t. ward 3 · file 013 · no signature

Patient No.: 013. Name: [ILLEGIBLE]. Status: Active. Follow-up note: The patient remains stable. No change in behavior. Still singing. Still smiling. Does not ask to leave. Does not ask about family. Shows no awareness of the passage of time. Recommendation: None. The patient is where she wants to be. [This note bears no signature and no date. The ink is fresh.]

Andrew & Luz · a margin note The file was there when we went up. Dr. Silvio says he didn’t write it. Luz says she didn’t either. Neither did I. The ink is from a modern Bic pen — not 1964 ink. The Academy of History of Magdalena has hospital records going back to 1804 and says private files were always lost. But this one wasn’t lost. It appeared. And now, every time we step into Ward 3, it’s open on the table. As if someone keeps leaving it for us to find. Academy of History of Magdalena
Document 12 · Current June 2026

Recommendation — Dr. Silvio · Principal Care

Current letterhead · Dr. Silvio’s signature · green ink el sanatorio s.a.s. · 2026-06

EL SANATORIO S.A.S. — Principal Care · Internal clinical memorandum · June 2026. Having reviewed the case of Patient 013, I recommend that her indefinite residence in this facility be maintained. The patient is where she wants to be. — Dr. Silvio, Principal Care, 2026.

Andrew & Luz · a margin note Colombian Law 1616 of 2013 says that mental health is a fundamental right and that every decision requires informed consent. But the law doesn’t speak about the patient whose only home was always the institution. How do you ask someone for consent to take her out of the only place she has ever known? Dr. Silvio handed us this note a week after the finding. It’s a single line. Luz read it and stayed quiet for a long time. Then she said: “He’s right.” And that was it. WHAT LUZ & I ARE READING

This story continues.

Sanatorio Varón is fiction. Calle 19 isn’t. La Monja del Pasillo isn’t. Pepe Vives isn’t. These are the archives we keep digging through. If you find something worth adding, write to us below — we’ll add it.

Hospital · 1746–1991

The Old Hospital San Juan de Dios

Santa Marta’s official hospital for over two centuries. The Brothers Hospitallers built it in 1746. In 1880 the nuns took over. In 1991 it was abandoned. That’s where the other story begins.

 Legend · since 1991

La Monja del Pasillo

A Sister of Charity. She fell for a doctor, wasn’t loved back, and hanged herself in one of the corridors of the San Juan de Dios. Watchmen say they still see her. So does Luz.

 Biography · 1894–1984

Pepe Vives de Andréis

Mayor, Governor, philanthropist, founder of El Informador in 1958 — and the man who finished the modern San Juan de Dios. His name appears in five of our margin notes.

 Reporting · 2025

Voices and apparitions at the San Juan de Dios

A recent report on the testimony of people who have entered the abandoned hospital. Babies’ cries, nuns with rosaries, doctors in shuttered operating rooms. Diario del Cauca, August 2025.

 National inventory

Haunted places of Colombia

The inventory gathered by Spanish Wikipedia. Santa Marta appears several times. Almost always the same building. Almost always the same nun.

 Archives · since 1804

Academy of History of Magdalena

Custodians of the Historical Archive of Magdalena Grande — notary records from 1804, when Santa Marta stretched all the way to Norte de Santander and part of Zulia. We’re there most Wednesdays.

 Conflict · 1948–1958

La Violencia

Nearly a million displaced. A quarter of a million dead. The backdrop to everything that happened on Calle 19 between 1947 and 1964.

 Psychiatry · 1930–1960

Heroic Therapies in Psychiatry

Insulin coma, electroshock, lobotomy. What was being done in earnest inside private clinics while the nuns at the official hospital said the rosary.

 UNESCO · Sierra Nevada

Mamos · Kogui · Arhuaco · Wiwa · Kankuamo

The elder brothers of the Sierra Nevada say that care does not require confinement. They also say that some souls stay in the places where they were loved. UNESCO wrote it down. It’s public information.

 Marijuana boom

Memorias de violencia · Universidad del Magdalena

The study that explains why nobody went back to Calle 19 for sixty years. JSTOR, open access.

 Folklore · Caribbean

Myths and legends of Colombia

La Llorona, La Madremonte, El Mohán — the figures the Caribbean has told about for centuries to explain what cannot be seen. The girl in Ward 3 fits into this album.

 Law · 2013

Mental Health Law 1616 — Colombia

The current legal framework. The right to informed consent, integrated care, dignity. Nothing about the patients whose only home was always the institution. That part still hasn’t been written.
